As the lone UX designer in a $450m+ company, I designed a client-facing enterprise project management system over roughly 3-5 months, all with little provided understanding of the problem space, limited resources, and a team unaccustomed to each other’s methods.

Early attempts to map out and understand the space

Through interviews, card sorting, combing through exchanged emails & files, complaint forms (read: anything I can get my hands on), I identified several pains that our clients and salespeople had to mutually endure, including:


 - Having no unified space to organize their projects, and

 - Having to use email and secure FTPs to generate and manage their projects and files


I also discovered that the file system was largely an ambitious nice-to-have from the business perspective. Half the product was not outright useful.


I dove into sketching. Along the way, I leaned on simple, conservative layouts but also suggested more technically complex solutions while challenging paradigms of existing features from the legacy product.


Demoing some of the solutions to my team lead and another PM. We had lengthy discussions about the many concepts behind the design, while exploring our technical capabilities on what’s feasible.


As this team was just being assembled, we spent the next couple of weeks onboarding new team members, to learn about their perspectives. Heading into (pre-planned) winter vacation, I was looking forward to collaborating with a new team and finally figuring out a lot of the still opaque mysteries behind this product in the new year.

Lots of data, not a whole lot of understanding

Our first strike: find out what ADM is from the stakeholder and the subject matter expert; in this case my team lead was both. We followed that up with interviewing our would-be support team in Boulder, remotely. Their support experience was actually for an entirely different product that served similar functions. The name: GLO.


We now have another product, and there was no single unified vision for what the original should be, while jogging alongside was history of another product with an entirely different client base.

300 fields, 60+ screens, mystery terminology and  dependencies. Lots of data, very little sense

Things we had #1: interested clients! Our main client-facing product went live. Now, a number of sizable client organizations were buying in and ready to be onboarded, and we had a team of support people ready to work.


Things we DIDN’T have #1: A tool to help our support team manage the large volume of organizational complexities that would surely accompany said clients.


Things we had #2: An unused legacy product that was our sole source of concrete details and functional specification. The name: ADM.


Things we DIDN’T have #2: Clear understanding of the exact details of how ADM *should* work! Picking it apart a bit. I inventoried the entirety of ADM. It turns out that there were over 300 different fields and controls, distributed across over 60 screens/sections, with five major sections to the application.


Hmmm

First thing to do? Talk to the people who did the work. (and claw away at getting client access)


I quickly organized and led interviews across different departments. One of the biggest revelations was that the word “revisions” had a history of being used for at least four different kinds of processes during our project lifecycle, all initiated by clients:


 - Changes to the original documents they wanted translated

 - Scheduled reviewing, and requesting changes of text strings mid-translation

 - Request to take documents from an older completed project, and iterating on the same “project” in some way shape or form

 - Requesting changes to deliverables for the project

 - And possibly more?


So we had a bit of a vocabulary problem.
